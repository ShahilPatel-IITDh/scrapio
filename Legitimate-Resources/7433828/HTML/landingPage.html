Halifax: House prices 'held up well' to fall just 1.3% in 2011, against tide of economic gloom

By Simon Lambert
Updated: 13:40 BST, 6 January 2012

161

View
comments

House prices held up well against a wave of economic gloom to fall by 1.3 per cent in 2011, Halifax has reported.

The major lender has forecast that property prices will continue to tread water this year but admitted that major storm clouds threaten.

Halifax said that house prices fell by 0.9 per cent in December to an average of 160,063, on its index, and were down 0.1 per cent in the final three months of the year.

Stalled: The property market has struggled but prices could have fallen further considering the economic gloom

House prices have now slipped back to the level seen in July 2009 and are 20 per cent off their 2007 peak on the index.

Martin Ellis, Halifaxs housing economist, said: If the UK can avoid recession, we expect broad stability in house prices in 2012. There is, however, considerable uncertainty regarding the prospects for the UK economy which will, to a large extent, depend on how events in the Eurozone unfold.

In addition, the extent to which households choose to reduce their debts will also affect growth. As a result, the outlook for house prices is also uncertain.

A further potential trap for property prices lies in banks increasing caution in the face of the Eurozone debt crisis and the potential losses the financial sector faces from it.

A pricey property decade

Despite having fallen substantially from the 199,600 August 2007 peak, house prices are still up 62 per cent on ten years ago, Halifax's figures show.

The Bank of England has warned that while lenders are likely to introduce lower deposit mortgages and innovative products to seemingly help first-time buyers or those with low equity, they will get tougher to get as lending criteria and credit scoring is tightened further.

Halifaxs annual house price measure compares average three-monthly figures from now and a year ago, which it says eases volatility. In a direct comparison to December 2010 when the average price stood at 163,665, house prices are down 2.2 per cent.

While the fall in prices has been relatively modest over the past 12 months, the real value of property is falling much faster due to high inflation eating into it. Consumer Prices Index inflation averaged 4.5 per cent from January to November (the latest figure), while Retail Prices Index inflation, which includes housing costs, averaged 5.25 per cent.

Enlarge

Steadying: Both Nationwide and Halifax have shown house prices treading water


What will happen to house prices in 2012

Halifax forecast last month that house prices would end the year within a range of two per cent down to two per cent up, as low transaction levels and continuing rock bottom interest rates kept a floor under the market.

Its prediction joined a slew of downbeat forecasts, with the the overwhelming message being that the property market faces another tough 12 months in 2012.

While none predict major falls, the general suggestion is of a flat market or house prices dipping slightly. If prices do remain steady or slip back, a decline in real terms adjusted for inflation will continue.

The Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors 2012 forecast is for a three per cent fall and its opening line sums up the doldrums the property market finds itself stuck in. Sales of homes may rise a little over the coming year but prices will struggle to follow suit, it said.

Nationwide sees house prices moving sideways or drifting modestly lower, while property information specialist Hometrack says they will fall three per cent.

Others are more pessimistic: Howard Archer, at IHS Global Insight , sees a five per cent fall, Capital Economics also predicts a five per cent fall, as does upmarket estate agent Knight Frank , and property expert Henry Pryor , suggests prices may fall 10 per cent.

Estate agents' view: There is no national housing market

Peter Bolton King, chief executive of the National Association of Estate Agents says: 'Whilst Halifaxs overall UK figure is no doubt statistically correct, it needs to be remembered that there is no such thing as a national housing market in the UK.

'Instead its a landscape of micro-markets, with high demand for property in some districts helping to maintain strong prices, but a stark division with less desirable areas where prices are certainly falling.

'Even towns and cities have micro-markets across different neighbourhoods. Thats why we recommend that buyers and sellers appoint estate agents who are licensed NAEA members to represent them, ensuring they receive a professional service, as well as protection under our Rules of Conduct.'

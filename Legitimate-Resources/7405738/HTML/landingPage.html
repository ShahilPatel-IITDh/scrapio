WHY THE 'BEST' EDUCATION ISN'T ALWAYS BEST FOR YOU



A VCE score of 90+ opens many doors but it is a mistake for successful students to be seduced only by prestige and status.

Year 12 students are often encouraged to choose their university course based on the institution's reputation, but at what cost?

As I write, VCE students across Australia are busting their academic chops to prepare for their final exams in the hopes of getting into their dream university. When year 12s have the chance to change their tertiary course preferences after their ATARs are released on December 14, some will choose their course based on the school's prestige and perceived status.

What will I say on my resume? 'Hi, I don't know much about this industry, but how about you hire me for some top-notch Marxism banter?'

While getting into an elite university might make the turmoil of  year 12 seem worthwhile – and it will definitely impress the relatives – you might be missing out on entering a course that's best for you.
As many high-achieving students do, I chose my university preferences with an air of ATAR snobbery, only considering courses from prestigious universities that my parents could brag about and teachers expected me to attend. I, like many of my friends, was enticed by the University of Melbourne for its ranking as the No. 1 university in Australia.

As a high school student with little clue about how to otherwise judge a university, I wrongly assumed that being No. 1 meant that it was the best possible place for me. So I knuckled down in year 12 to score a 90+ ATAR, earning myself a golden ticket to Australia's most prestigious educational institution.
Proudly placing my student card into the clear display pocket of my wallet for cashiers to notice whenever I opened it at the shops, I joined the elite cohort of Melbourne University. I began an arts degree, hoping to pursue a career in journalism, much to my mother's delight. However, it wasn't long before I started to reconsider my decision of doing a prestige degree.

Amidst the chatter of well-meaning teachers and relatives impressed upon hearing about my university entry, I realised that I had failed to check out the actual nuts-and-bolts details of my chosen course. In the faint distance, you could hear the Melbourne Model laughing at my naivety in between sips of a slow-roasted almond milk latte.

Introduced by vice-chancellor Glyn Davis in 2008, the Melbourne Model imitates the European/US tertiary system where 96 specialised undergraduate degrees were replaced by a few broader "new generation" degrees including art, science and commerce. This new way of structuring undergraduate study was meant to create a "distinctive Melbourne experience".

Well, distinctive is one way to put it.

Stephen King, a dean from Monash University called this model "one of the best things to ever happen to Monash" as would-be Melbourne students turned to Monash for more conventional, specialised bachelor degrees. I soon started to understand why Monash was rejoicing in the flaws of Melbourne's new structure.

For media & communication majors like myself, my course offers just two journalism-specific subjects throughout the entire three-year degree. With an arts degree under my belt after graduation, I'll be nowhere near qualified to enter the professional workforce.

What will I say on my resume? "Hi, I don't know much about this industry, but how about you hire me for some top-notch Marxism banter?"

Don't get me wrong; these broad "new generation" degrees are highly suitable for those who want to study a wide array of humanities/science/commerce-based subjects before undertaking a specialised postgraduate – they're just not for everyone.

Also, I don't have a cool $40,000 sitting in my gold-encrusted wallet to pay for postgraduate study, so I probably shouldn't have done a course that relies upon me doing further study.

Alas, it hit me – my course preferences were blinded by the glimmer of prestige, and I've ended up in a course that isn't right for me.

How did I end up making this huge mistake?
Don't blame the University of Melbourne, as it's a great choice for people who want to (and can afford to) explore an array of disciplines in a generalist degree before doing a postgrad. Blame the "culture of prestige" for making us prioritise a school's status before our own unique needs.

I'm a casualty of this culture of prestige, where bright-minded high school students are advised by parents, teachers and career advisers to not "waste their ATAR" by choosing a course with low ATAR requirements. Many of my university friends have similar stories of career counsellors advising them to list schools with the highest status as their top preferences, to avoid "wasting" their hard-earned score.
Walk into any biomedicine or law lecture, and you're sure to find at least one person who doesn't even want to be a doctor or lawyer, but fell victim to the pressures of "not letting that 99 ATAR go to waste".
Although this advice is given with the best of intentions, it leaves many young people trapped in the wrong course and eventually having to start afresh with some additional HECS debt to their name. With one in five undergraduate students changing or dropping out of their courses within the first year of study, something is definitely going wrong.

So let's stop buying into the myth that prestige is always best. Just because a school is ranked highly doesn't necessarily mean it's the best choice for you, your student or your child.

Instead, think carefully about your prospective schools. How are their degrees structured? What are the institution's strong points? What type of subjects will your course offer? And most importantly, does your course allow you to follow your passion?
​
So, when Year 12s have the chance to change their VTAC course preferences after ATARs are released in December, my advice for them is to focus less on status and more on a school's suitability to you. Remember – not all that glitters is gold.
